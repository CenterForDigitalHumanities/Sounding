Broken Books Description

Often times in history books are taken apart, "broken" into pieces and dispersed. Our site allows for the virtual reconstruction of one of these broken books, the pre-modern manuscript known as the Llangattock Breviary and continues to be developed so that any book can be digitally reconstructed.

Deriving its nickname from a later owner, John Allan Rolls, the 1st Baron Llangattock, the Breviary was sold at Christie's, London in 1958. After the sale, this manuscript was broken apart and the separated leaves were sold on the American market by a dealer from Boston. To view the Digital Scriptorium implementation of this digital object, you can go to OMEKA here.

Working with collaborative partners, including many members of Digital Scriptorium, we have collected digital images of this book's leaves from institutions and private collections all over the world, including Harvard, U.C. The power behind the digital reconstruction is shared canvas technology and the International Image Interoperability Framework (IIIF). Using these innovative tools we can create canvases that hold the digital images of the relevant leaves and annotatations to store metadata about them. These canvases will be grouped in pairs so that users can attach annotations, including cataloging metadata, to individual images or to a whole leaf, with the goal of virtually reconstructing the original manuscript.

One component of Broken Books is the ability for anyone to contribute at any level they want. The interface offers a multitude of fields for metadata, image upload ability and the ability to place the leaf within a structure an administrator has prepared. Users are required to submit at minimum ONE piece of information about the leaf they are suggesting, which means you can fill out as much or as little information about the leaf as feels comfortable.
